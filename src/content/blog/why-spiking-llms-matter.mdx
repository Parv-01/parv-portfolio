# Why Spiking LLMs Matter

The defining constraint of large-model deployment is **energy per token**, not flops per token. A 70B dense transformer doing inference on H100s runs in the **hundreds of watts per request** territory and that's before you count the cooling pull at rack level.

Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) borrow the brain's tactic: emit a 1-bit spike only when a neuron's membrane potential crosses threshold. Most of the time, most of the neurons are silent. On neuromorphic hardware like Intel's Loihi 2 or SpiNNaker, that silence translates to *actual zero energy* -> not "low" energy, *zero*.

## The catch

Surrogate-gradient training is brittle. Backprop through a non-differentiable spike function needs a smooth proxy in the backward pass, and small changes to that proxy shift the loss landscape unpredictably. We've seen 3-4% accuracy swings on identical models with identical hyperparameters.

## What I'm trying

A hybrid: keep dense attention blocks (they encode positional information densely), swap the MLP/FFN sub-blocks for spiking variants trained with surrogate gradients. Distill from a frozen LLaMA teacher to skip the cold-start problem.

> Early result: 89.6% of teacher BLEU on a translation task, at a projected 8× energy reduction on Loihi 2 simulator.

The number that matters isn't 89.6%. It's the *projected* 8×->because if it holds on real silicon (and that's a big *if*), the deployment economics flip.

## Where I'd start if you wanted to dabble

- snnTorch's tutorial on surrogate gradients
- Norse for PyTorch-native SNNs
- NEST for spiking simulation (CPU-only, but rock solid)
- Intel's Lava framework for Loihi 2 (steep learning curve)
